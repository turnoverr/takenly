The user stories
Two distinct journeys that Takenly is built to serve simultaneously
Margaret, 71
The Patient
Takes 4 medications across 3 daily slots. Her daughter lives interstate. She has an iPhone but rarely uses apps. She sometimes forgets morning meds, occasionally doubles up on evening ones.

What she needs: Something that works without any setup. A big button. A clear confirmation. Nothing complicated.

What she currently uses: A paper chart stuck to the fridge. It works until it doesn't.
Sarah, 41
The Carer
Margaret's daughter. She calls every evening to check. She's set up three different reminder apps for her mum — none have lasted more than a week. She can't see whether meds were actually taken.

What she needs: To configure everything herself, remotely. To know it happened. To stop worrying.

What she currently uses: Phone calls. Anxiety.

The Takenly journey
How Sarah and Margaret use the product together
Step 1 — Sarah sets up Margaret's schedule (5 minutes)
Sarah opens Takenly on her own phone. Selects "Someone I care for." Chooses morning, lunch, and bedtime slots. Enters Margaret's medications and doses for each slot. Sets the times. Gets a shareable link.
Step 2 — Margaret receives the link
Sarah sends the link via iMessage. Margaret taps it. She sees a simple view — just her name, today's schedule, and one big "Mark as taken" button per slot. No setup. No account. No decisions.
Step 3 — Margaret marks doses taken
When it's time, Margaret sees the slot highlighted. She taps the button. The screen flashes green — unmissable confirmation. The dose is logged.
Step 4 — Sarah sees it happened (coming Sprint 9)
Sarah's dashboard shows Margaret's adherence in real time. She gets a push notification when a dose is marked. She stops calling every evening to check.

The only app designed to be configured by a carer and used by a patient
Medisafe's Medfriend is notification-only — the patient still configures everything. MedAdvisor is a pharmacy platform. No competitor has separated the configuration role from the usage role.
Design principles — non-negotiable
Colour discipline
Teal = interactive only. Green = taken/success. Red = missed. Amber = early/partial/late. Held consistently across all 30+ versions with zero semantic bleed.
Fraunces serif — used in exactly three places
The 52px due-time display. The adherence ring percentage. The streak count. Everything else is DM Sans. This discipline is what makes the due-time card the most memorable moment in the app.
No complexity for the patient
The patient view has no tab bar, no settings, no history, no navigation. One action per slot. Full-screen confirmation. Works for a 75-year-old who rarely uses apps.

v3.x architecture — what Firebase unlocks
The architecture shift
v1–v2 were localStorage-only — all state lived in the browser, one device, no sync. v3.x adds the cloud layer. Firebase Firestore becomes the source of truth. The patient link goes from "same browser only" to "any device, real time." This is the most important architectural change in the product's history — it's what makes the carer-primary model actually work across two different iPhones.
